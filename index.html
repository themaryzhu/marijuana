How We See And Use Marijuana

By MEDHA VERMA and MARY ZHU          MAR. 27, 2020

Marijuana is the most commonly used psychotropic drug in the United States, after alcohol. Made from the dried stems and leaves of the Cannabis sativa or Cannabis indica plant, the drug’s allure comes from its mind-altering primary chemical THC. It was first outlawed for any use in 1970 under the Controlled Substances Act, but was eventually legalized in some states for only medical purposes in 1990. Since 2010, multiple states have legalized the recreational use of marijuana, stimulating its rising popularity in the U.S.
With the legalization of marijuana in multiple states and the increase in usage, we were curious to delve into how the perceptions and usage of marijuana have changed. Do perceptions of the risks of marijuana influence its actual usage? How does usage differ between legal-use states and illegal-use states? In today's age, how do factors like gender, race, and education impact how quickly people start using marijuana? By the end of this article, we hope to show you how Americans see and use marijuana in the 21st century.

As we progress with older age ranges, we notice more marijunana users in the 18-25 age range, and the most marijuana users in the oldest age range (26+ years old). Although this may simply be a byproduct of the oldest age group being the largest, we do see that most users across America are adult age or older.
When looking at marijuana usage and states where marijuana is legal for recreational use, we find a positive relationship. Among all eight jurisdictions where recreational marijuana is legal (Alabama, California, Colorado, Washington, D.C., Maine, Massachusetts, Nevada, and Washington), the average percentage of marijuana users is ~17%, while the average among illegal states is ~12%.


After studying how factors like age and legality influence usage, we became curious about exploring first time use of marijuana. What triggers people to start using marijuana in the first place?

U.S. First Time Marijuana Use Considered Based on Sex, Education, and Race

Immediately, we see that race plays a signficant role in first time use. Overall, Caucasians have a significantly higher count for first time use. It's also visible that Caucasians are actually likely to try marijuana for the first time in the their late twenties and onwards, wheras other races aren't. On the other hand, Asians have a very low first time usage count over all ages, and the majority try it for the first time between the ages of 15 and 20.
We also see that education impacts the age of first time use. For instance, high school graduates and people with some type of associate/college degree have very similar trends for their first time use of marijuana, with the peak being around 10-13 years old. However, for high school dropouts we see that the peak is closer to 14-15, the age we start high school. And finally, for college graduates, the peak is around 20, which is significantly older.
On the other hand, when it came to gender, we see that the curve is almost identical for men and women, with first time use being slightly earlier for men.


Take-Aways

As marijuana becomes more and more mainstream within the U.S. despite its associated health risks, it's important to examine how and why this phenomenon is occurring.
In this article, we examine several factors that influence marijuana use, and emerge with several new insights. First, we see that in states where the recreational use of marijuana is legal, both perceptions of risk associated with monthly marijuana use are lower, and the actual usage of marijuana is higher. As a result, the government plays a signficant role in sending signals concerning marijuana through the power of its jurisdiction. Next, we see that the vast majority of current marijuana users are of adult age or older. This insight is a valuable stepping stone for further questions regarding cannabis use among different age groups - what causes older individuals to start using marijuana? What is the average age of all users? Finally, we see demographics play a large role in influencing the first-time use of marijuana. Caucasians and college-educated individuals have higher first-time use counts, while surprisingly, there is no signficant difference among gender.
To some, marijuana is a "dangerous gateway drug." To others, marijuana is nothing but a "harmless hobby." We can only hope to gather more insights from the data available to us as this debate continues.
